02

How do you approach building a product from scratch?

Product management isn’t about writing requirements — it’s about identifying the right problem before building the solution.

I always begin with understanding the problem rather than jumping to solutions. Before creating a roadmap, I spend time understanding customers, business goals, existing workflows, operational pain points, and technical constraints. I like talking to users, reviewing support tickets, analysing data, and challenging assumptions until I’m confident we’ve identified the root problem rather than its symptoms.

Only then do I define the product vision, success metrics, and roadmap. From there, I work with engineering and stakeholders to break the vision into incremental releases that deliver value quickly while leaving room to learn and iterate. One thing I’ve learned over the years: customers rarely ask for the right solution — they describe their frustrations. It’s our responsibility as Product Managers to uncover the real opportunity behind those frustrations.

My product principles

Customer FirstData-Informed DecisionsOutcome over OutputIterative Delivery

03

How do you make product decisions when information is incomplete?

Every Product Manager operates with uncertainty. Waiting for perfect information usually means missing the opportunity.

I don’t believe perfect information exists. Whether I was prioritising integrations for American Express’s India Data Localization program or defining the roadmap for Program Hierarchy, there were always competing priorities, changing requirements, and unknowns.

My approach is to reduce uncertainty rather than eliminate it. I gather enough information to understand customer impact, business value, technical complexity, regulatory considerations, and engineering effort. Then I make a decision, communicate the reasoning transparently, and remain willing to adjust based on new information. Good Product Managers don’t avoid ambiguity — they become comfortable making thoughtful decisions within it.

My product principles

Prioritise LearningMake Reversible Decisions QuicklyCommunicate Trade-offsAdapt with Evidence

04

How do you balance business goals with technical constraints?

Product Managers sit between business and engineering. Success depends on translating both perspectives into a shared direction.

I don’t see business and engineering as opposing forces. Business teams are trying to solve customer problems; engineering teams are trying to build sustainable systems. My role is to align both around a common outcome.

When trade-offs arise, I focus on three questions: Does this solve a meaningful customer problem? Is it technically sustainable? Does it create measurable business value? I’ve found that involving engineering early in product discovery leads to better ideas, fewer surprises, and stronger product decisions than treating engineering as an execution team.

My product principles

Build with Engineering, Not for EngineeringTechnical Feasibility MattersCustomer Value Comes FirstLong-Term Scalability

05

How do you think about platform products and ecosystem strategy?

Many of the products I’ve worked on weren’t standalone applications — they were platforms supporting multiple business functions, markets, and integrations.

Platform products require a different mindset. Instead of thinking about one feature or one customer journey, I think about how different products, teams, and systems interact as an ecosystem. Whether modernising Program Hierarchy across 22 markets or leading integrations for India’s Data Localization program, my focus has always been on building reusable capabilities rather than one-off solutions.

A well-designed platform enables future innovation because every new capability builds on a strong foundation instead of introducing more complexity. That’s why I enjoy platform product management — it requires thinking beyond today’s roadmap and designing for tomorrow’s possibilities.

My product principles

Build Reusable CapabilitiesReduce ComplexityThink in SystemsDesign for Scale

06

What does successful product leadership look like to you?

Product Managers rarely succeed through authority alone. Leadership is measured by the ability to align people around a shared vision.

For me, leadership is less about making every decision and more about creating clarity. The best Product Managers I’ve worked with ask thoughtful questions, encourage diverse perspectives, and create an environment where engineering, design, business, and leadership feel ownership of the product.

Throughout my career, I’ve rarely had formal authority over the people delivering a product. Success has depended on building trust, communicating transparently, and helping stakeholders understand why certain trade-offs create better long-term outcomes. When teams align around the customer problem instead of individual opinions, execution becomes significantly easier.

My leadership principles

Influence Without AuthorityTransparencyOwnershipEmpower TeamsContinuous Learning
